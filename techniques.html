Techniques

USE YOUR FEET YOUR

Feet are the foundation of climbing. A lot of beginners try to pull themselves up the wall and get tired quickly. Think about climbing a ladder â€“ you shouldn't be pulling yourself up, you're stepping up and using your hands for balance. This is the same when climbing a mountain.

MAINTAIN BLANCE

When you have to use a side grip, you can pull straight down. So you need to find a way to resist that side's pull, so you don't lose your balance and fall off the wall.

SEE MORE

HOW TO CLIMB MORE EFFICIENTLY

Straight arms will help you feel less tired. Straightening your arms allows your skeleton to take up most of the weight, not your muscles. Even a slight bend in your elbow means your muscles are working to keep it in that position.

SEE MORE

STEP BACK

A step back is the opposite of a normal step. Instead of stepping on one toe with your big toe and hip perpendicular to the wall, you rotate your hip to the side and step with the outside edge (side of the little toe) of the shoe.

SEE MORE

KNEES DOWN

Lowering the knee is a bigger step backwards. It works best when you have a standing position near hip level. Step on it with your toes; then roll your knee inward until the outside of your shoe rests on the sole and your knee is actually pointing down.

SEE MORE

INSET LEGS

You can use any combination of arms and legs, but the key is to use counterweight pressure for balance. Foot inserts depend on your calves, so it's an extremely effective way to climb, and can give you some great resting positions.

SEE MORE

HANGING PEOPLE

Hanging is advantageous whenever you use protrusions located on the same side of the body. Because that also shifts all your weight to one side, you can simply rotate that leg to the other side of your body to keep yourself balanced.

SEE MORE

BASED ON

Leaning is when you pull and lean against one side of a rock or crack and push your foot into the other side. When you have good footing, leaning posture is very effective because your arms are straight and your feet do the hard work. When a lack of good footing requires you to exert yourself, keep the heel low to maximize the amount of rubber against the kick.

SEE MORE

MANTLE POSE

Mantle is a pose where you bend down and bring your legs up to touch your hands. A classic example of a mantle is at the top of a climb when you need to pull yourself up a ledge. To mantle, press down and hold to have your weight on it, then move your legs up to alternate your hands.

SEE MORE

STICK TO THE BOTTOM

Like the name implies, bottom grip is when you use the lower body. Using the lower body can feel counter-intuitive at first, because instead of pulling down and holding, you're pulling up. One key to this pose is finding a good, tall stand so you can maintain the tension of your body by pushing with your feet as you hold. Keeping your feet high places the hold around waist level and allows you to keep your arms straighter.

SEE MORE

DRAG TO LANDSCAPE

Pulling sideways is using any part of the body to pull to the side. The key to well-executed nagng traction is by varying your body weight or by using a counterforce.

SEE MORE

GASTON

Gaston is the inverse of lateral drag. In a side pull, the hold is oriented so you pull in, towards you. The gaston pose is also oriented for a horizontal force, but instead of pulling in, it requires pushing out.
