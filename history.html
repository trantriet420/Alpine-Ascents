History

You may be surprised, but extreme mountain climbing is one of the activities of mankind since ancient times with a lot of history.

Modern recreational climbing was actually developed and started at the end of the 19th century. But breakthroughs stemming from new technologies and tactics have made it one of the most widely known disciplines around the world. world.

More specifically, climbing has officially debuted in the Tokyo 2020 Olympics with the expectation of many international competitors at high levels. Thousands of enthusiasts have been introduced to the sport thanks to the burgeoning indoor climbing fitness industry. Meanwhile, more and more elite mountaineers continue to travel to the ends of the earth in search of the "untouched mountain".

To understand what people are really doing, let's intoWild go through the history of rock climbing and the language that evolved around it.

1. An ancient activity

There is a lot of evidence that ancient and pre-modern cultures around the globe actually practiced and experienced this "engineering topography". In Mustang, Nepal, archaeologists have discovered a series of intricate burial caves that can only be reached by climbing the face of dramatic steep cliffs. The American Southwest, meanwhile, has ample evidence of indigenous tribes living among the region's mesa cliffs. Most of the ancient people probably climbed for safety.

Three places are said to be the birthplace of modern recreational rock climbing: the Peak and Lake Districts in England, the Elbe Sandstone region in southeastern Germany, and the Dolomites Mountains in Northern Italy. In the first decade of the 20th century, pioneers experimented with first-generation climbing inventions such as steel carabiners and soft iron ring pistons.

2. Safe device

Freestyle climbing does not mean climbing without safety equipment such as ropes.

Instead, freestyle climbing means that the climber will attempt to reach his or her target peak using only their body. Safety systems such as ropes or rock cushions can be used in the event of a fall, but not to aid in the ascent. In contrast, assisted climbing means that the climber uses a variety of technical equipment to carefully pull himself up the cliff.

Since the earliest days of climbing, climbers have debated the stylistic intricacies involved in that distinction. In 1911, the famous German climber Paul Preuss published an essay in the German Alpine Journal in which he criticized the top climbers of the day for using pitons to pull themselves up high mountains. the road that they cannot climb with just physical ability. “Piton is an emergency aid, not the basis of a climbing system,” he wrote, sparking one of the community's first major debates over style.

During the first decades of the 20th century, techniques and knowledge developed in the original hotspots traveled from across North America, Europe, and other countries. This early period culminated in the 1930s, when many watershed engineering climbs were made across Europe and North America, from the first ascent to Ship Rock in New Mexico and Devil's Tower in Wyoming, to the Walker Spur of the Grandes Jorasses in the French Alps.

While most rock climbers are drawn to the biggest and most obvious challenges, a small group of free climbers are focused on mastering their athletic potential with goals. smaller pepper.

4. The birth of the giant wall

The massive granite walls in Yosemite Valley, California, became the most important rock climbing test ground in the post-World War II era. In the late 1940s, Swiss immigrant and master blacksmith John Salathe began experimenting with a new piston made of extremely hard steel that could be hammered into the thin cracks of Yosemite and then removed for Continue use without warping. Salathe's new designs, along with a host of other inventions including mechanical lifts and specialized hammocks that could be anchored to sheer cliff faces, rekindled the golden age of Yosemite climbing, in It is home to many of the area's most iconic cliffs, including Half Dome and El Capitan, which were ascended for the first time using assisted climbing techniques.

5. Road to the Olympics

From informal local events to a wide range of international activities, climbing gyms have fostered the growth of climbing as a legally organized sport. Early events such as the 1988 International Sport Climbing Competition, held on a 33-meter-high outdoor wall next to a motel in Snowbird, Utah, helped pave the way for the nationally recognized IFSC World Cup series. recognized internationally and eventually climbing was included in the 2020 Olympics in Tokyo.

For the Olympic debut of rock climbing, competitors will compete in three disciplines: rock climbing, sport lead climbing and speed climbing, for a medal. In future Olympics, many athletes hope to see each sport receive its own medal distinction.

Much of the future of climbing lies in large areas of the world that the first climbers have not systematically explored.
